You're not overcomplicating the *idea* — a customer portal is a real feature that real invoicing products have. But for ClearContract v1, I'd say don't build it yet, and here's the reasoning. (Worth noting: your own plan already made this call — "customer accounts/logins" is explicitly in the Out of Scope list in section 9.)

**The core value is mostly already in your architecture.** Every quote and invoice gets a long-lived public token link (`/q/[token]`, `/i/[token]`). The customer clicks a link in their email and sees the document, can accept, pay a deposit, pay a balance — no login, no password, no friction. That token-link model is doing 80% of what a portal does, and it's doing it *better* for your actual audience, because...

**Think about who the customer is.** For a driveway or landscaping business, the typical customer gets one or two quotes from you *ever*. They will not create an account, they will not remember a password, and a login wall in front of a quote is friction that directly hurts your acceptance rate — which is the metric the whole product is built around. A portal is valuable when customers have an ongoing relationship with lots of documents (think property managers, landlords, commercial clients with repeat jobs). Most of your users' customers aren't that.

**What a portal actually costs you.** It's not just a page — it's a second auth system alongside the trader auth you already have: customer registration, password resets, email verification, linking accounts to customer records (what happens when the same person has two email addresses across two quotes?), new PocketBase collections and API rules, and a bunch of new security surface. That's easily a phase-sized chunk of work (a week+), competing with Phases 3–8 which are the stuff that makes the product usable at all.

**If you want the portal feeling without the portal cost**, there's a nice middle path that fits your existing design: a customer-scoped token page. Give each `customers` record its own `portal_token`, and a public page at `/c/[token]` that lists all that customer's quotes and invoices (reusing the same sanitized-payload hook pattern you already have for `/q/[token]`). Link to it from the footer of quote/invoice emails as "View all your documents." No accounts, no passwords, no new auth system — maybe a day or two of work instead of a week, and it slots in cleanly after Phase 6 (invoices) since before then there's nothing to list. If you want a touch more security later, gate it behind a magic link (enter your email, get a fresh link) rather than passwords.

**My recommendation:** park it. Ship through Phase 8, quote real jobs, and watch for the actual signal — a repeat customer (or one of your future users) asking "where can I see all my invoices?" If that happens, build the token-based customer page first, and only graduate to real customer logins if you land users whose clients are landlords/property managers with genuinely ongoing relationships.
