So the idea is: instead of (or on top of) the one-off quote/invoice links, a customer gets an account with you and can log in to see every quote and invoice you've ever sent them. The problem it solves is "where did that link go?" — customers digging through email for the quote you sent three weeks ago.

Worth saying first: the plan already made a call on this. "Customer accounts/logins" is explicitly in the Out of Scope list (section 9), alongside the decision that public pages are token-based, not auth-based. That doesn't mean the idea is wrong — but it means you'd be reversing a deliberate decision, so the bar is evidence, not vibes. And the other relevant fact: the repo has zero commits. You're at Phase 0 of a ~4–6 week plan whose heart (the quote builder, public page, payments) doesn't exist yet.

The steelman version of your idea is real, though: a repeat customer — a landlord with three properties, a builder who subs work to you — genuinely benefits from one place with everything. And "your quotes live in a portal" does look professional. The instinct is good. The framing that's off is the *login*.

Here's the pressure test. Your end customer is a homeowner getting a driveway done. They will receive, in their entire relationship with you, maybe one quote and one invoice. Asking them to create an account — password, verification email, forgotten-password flow — to see two documents is friction exactly where the plan says friction is fatal: the public page is your storefront, and every extra step before "Accept & Pay Deposit" costs you conversions. Meanwhile the build cost is the worst kind: a second auth surface. A new customer-auth collection (your `customers` records aren't auth records), signup/login/reset flows, linking accounts to customer records across traders (same email, two traders — now what?), new API rules, and a whole new class of security tests. Honestly 5–8 days for one part-time dev, plus a permanent security surface to maintain. That's more than Phase 4 and Phase 6 combined, for a feature most of your customers would use once.

Three real options:

1. **Do nothing (defer).** The email link *is* the portal for a one-job customer. The token pages already work logged-out on a phone. Cost: 0 days.
2. **Tokenized customer page, no login.** A `/c/[token]` page — same pattern as `/q/[token]` — listing that customer's quotes and invoices with links. One `portal_token` field on `customers`, one public hook endpoint returning a sanitized list, one page. Fits the existing architecture perfectly, roughly 1–2 days including tests, zero passwords. Every quote/invoice email footer gets "View all your documents from James Driveways." That's 90% of your idea for 20% of the cost.
3. **Full login portal.** The 5–8 day version above. Only defensible if you someday serve customers with many concurrent jobs (property managers) — a different market than the sole-trader-quoting-homeowners one you're building for.

My recommendation: option 1 now, with option 2 as the earmarked upgrade. You haven't shipped a single quote yet — you have no evidence customers lose links, because you have no customers. Build Phases 0–6 first. If real users start emailing "can you resend that invoice?", that's your signal, and the token-page version slots in after Phase 6 without touching the schema beyond one field. The login version I'd say no to outright — it fights the token-based architecture the plan chose, and it taxes the exact moment (customer opens quote, accepts, pays) the product lives or dies on.
