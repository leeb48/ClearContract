So the question is: does the public quote page need a proper e-signature step at acceptance, or is the button click legally solid enough to launch with? Worth saying up front — the plan already made a call on this: quote e-signatures are explicitly in the Out of Scope list (section 9). Your mate's comment is a good reason to re-examine that call, not automatically overturn it.

First, the legal bit, because your mate's instinct is common but mostly wrong for the UK. A contract needs offer, acceptance, consideration, and intention — none of which requires a signature, wet or electronic. Click-to-accept is enforceable, and courts care far less about *how* acceptance was expressed than about whether you can *prove* what was accepted, by whom, and when. And here's the kicker: your design already produces better evidence than most paper quotes ever do. The snapshot freezes exactly what the customer saw (line items, T&Cs, totals) at send time; `quote_events` logs the accept with IP/user-agent and timestamp; `accepted_total` is locked server-side; and if they pay a deposit through Stripe, money changed hands — the strongest acceptance evidence that exists. A scrawl on a phone screen adds nothing a judge would value on top of that.

Where your mate has a real point is *psychology and evidence quality on the non-card paths*. "Accept — pay cash" is one anonymous tap. If a dispute comes six months later, "someone clicked a button" is thinner than "someone typed their full name and ticked a box agreeing to the T&Cs." Signing also makes customers feel more committed, which matters on £10k driveways.

So three options:

1. **Do nothing** — click acceptance plus the existing event log. Legally fine, zero cost. Defensible, but leaves the cash/bank-transfer accepts feeling anonymous.
2. **Typed-name acceptance** — before confirming, the customer types their full name and ticks "I agree to the terms of this quote." Store the name and T&C snapshot reference in the event meta (and an `accepted_by_name` field on the quote), then email a confirmation receipt to both parties. Here's the satisfying part: under UK law a typed name *is* an electronic signature — so your mate literally gets his signature, and you pay roughly half a day to a day inside Phase 4/5. One extra field in the accept payload, no new collections, no new statuses.
3. **Full e-signature** — drawn-signature canvas or DocuSign-style integration. Drawn signature means a canvas component, image storage, embedding it in confirmations/PDFs — call it 2–3 days — and it's genuinely fiddly on a phone. Third-party signing costs per-envelope money and bolts a clunky redirect onto the page that is your product's storefront. Every extra step on that page loses acceptances; this option trades real conversions for imaginary legal safety.

**My recommendation: option 2, and don't delay anything for it.** It's not really "adding e-signatures" — it's a name field and a checkbox on the accept flow you're building in Phase 4/5 anyway, so the Out of Scope entry barely bends. It converts your mate's concern into about a day of work, upgrades the weakest evidence path (cash accepts), and keeps the mobile flow one screen. Skip option 3 entirely unless a real customer someday disputes an accepted quote — that's the evidence that would justify revisiting it, and the schema won't fight you if it happens.

One caveat worth its own sentence: nothing is built yet — no commits — so "before I launch" is a Phase 4/5 decision you're making early, which is actually the ideal time to make it. Just fold it into the accept-endpoint design (body becomes `{method, name, agreed_terms}`, server rejects accepts without them) and add a test: accept without a name → 4xx.
